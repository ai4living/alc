HEalthcare AI


Identifying Alternate Level of Care (ALC) patients using AI

Funded by Research Nova Scotia

Objective

This project applies various machine learning techniques to hospital administrative data in order to identify ALC patients as early as their admission time, and, ultimately, to design a hospital support system that managers can use to better plan patient flow within their facilities.

What?

ALC patients are hospitalized patients who no longer require acute beds, and who are ready to be discharged to alternative non-acute health care providers, such as home care and long-term care facilities.

How?

We leverage machine learning algorithms to improve bed utilization planning and patient flow within hospitals by designing a decision support framework using machine learning algorithms

When?

This is a two-year research project that starts in March 2022.

Project Summary

Addressing ALC patient flow issues requires advanced modeling and planning tools that can capture the complexities of a healthcare system. This research project will focus on developing machine learning models and tools that will be able to capture these complexities, thereby enabling healthcare administrators to respond to ALC patient flows more effectively.

Shortage of beds in hospitals

A lack or shortage of available alternative beds can result in ALC patients remaining in hospitals for significant extended periods, sometimes years. However, This prolonged occupation of acute beds has several negative impacts on both patients and the healthcare system.

Delayed discharge of acute patients

Delayed discharge of acute patients can result in a shortage of acute beds—which is the main cause of congested wards and emergency departments—cancelled or delayed elective surgeries, and delays in ambulance offload.

ALC patients in Canada

The study of ALC patients is important because Canada and Nova Scotia are home to an aging population that will continue to grow in both number and age—albeit with a greater number of life-limiting conditions—due to improvements in medical treatments for chronic conditions.

Improving healthcare system

This project will help improve the healthcare system’s efficiency in Nova Scotia.

Prediction with AI

The machine learning models in this project will predict identification of ALC patients, and their length of stay.

Project collaborators and partners

We have collaborators from Saint Mary’s University, Dalhousie University, Nova Scotia Health - Central and Eastern Zones, Dartmouth General Hospital.

Project Findings

Alternate Level of Care Patients in Canada: a Scoping Review

Maisonnave, M., Rajabi, E., Taghavi, M., & VanBerkel, P. (2024). Alternate Level of Care Patients in Canada: a Scoping Review. Canadian Geriatrics Journal, 27(4), 519.

We highlighted how the ALC problem is increasing healthcare costs, disrupting services, increasing waiting times, and compromising access to valuable resources. We found evidence suggesting that this far-reaching crisis affects the patients, their families, and the medical personnel. We discovered several logistical issues (such as access to long-term care) affecting the patient’s hospital length of stay.
